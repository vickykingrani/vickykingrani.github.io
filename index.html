Case 01 (continued) · Qualitative observation

Three patterns from contextual research with senior analysts

Three weeks observing analysts during their end-of-day risk runs, supplemented by the eleven structured interviews. Three patterns surfaced consistently — each pinpointing where the survey complaints actually came from.

The hierarchy node picker was a modal that obscured the analysis page.

Selecting a portfolio meant losing visibility of what was already configured. Analysts opened and closed the modal repeatedly to verify their selections before running a single compute.

Risk metric selection was a flat list of hundreds of metric variants with no grouping or defaults.

Selecting the right combination required institutional memory. New joiners could not operate the tool independently without a senior analyst alongside them.

Bookmarks lived in a separate dialog, disconnected from the analysis flow.

As a result, the bookmarking feature was largely unused. Analysts re-entered their parameters daily.

Every analyst, without exception, was exporting to Excel for the analysis the platform should have been supporting. The tool wasn't extending the workflow — it was the bottleneck the workflow had to route around.

08 · 01 / 07
Case 01 (continued) · Personas, alignment & strategy

From research insight to design strategy

Three things needed to land before the redesign could begin: who the system was actually serving, which competing stakeholders had to be aligned, and what design principles would guide every decision that followed.

Two users, one static screen

The contextual research surfaced a fundamental clash. The legacy interface was forcing two entirely different personas onto the exact same static form.

The Daily Analyst

Needed five inputs to run a standard overnight VaR report. Configuration was largely the same most days; the rest of the form was noise.

The Methodology Expert

Needed 50+ inputs to run deep, exploratory "What-If" stress scenarios. Required unfiltered access to the full parameter space.

It's a generic solution with everything in one application. If you split it into 4+ different applications — analytics, reporting, etc. — that delivered exactly what the user wanted.
Senior risk analyst — survey response

The respondent had articulated the architecture before I designed it. The strategy followed from there: don't delete complex features, make the system respond to user context — and split it where the work actually splits.

Three principles for what came next

Contextual loading

Static forms become responsive. The UI updates fields, components, and portfolios based on the metric selected — masking different backend calculators.

Progressive disclosure

Hide advanced parameters by default. Smart chips and accordions reveal only what is relevant to the analyst's current task.

In-app collaboration

Eliminate offline file sharing. Analysts save, manage, and share query bookmarks securely within the platform itself.

10 · 01 / 07
Case 01 (continued) · Adaptive behaviour & personalisation

An interface that adapts to scenario and to user

Risk analytics workflows vary enormously by scenario. FRTB calculations, VaR scenarios, and stress testing each need different inputs, and on the backend they run through different calculators. The frontend's job was to handle that without forcing the analyst to think about it.

Smart defaults on the empty state

The Analysis page now opens with VaR Type, Risk Class, Currency, and Reporting prepopulated to the analyst's last-used values. Returning users configure only what has changed; new joiners get a sensible starting point.

Adaptive Portfolio Definition

When VaR, SVaR, or stress metrics are selected, a different Portfolio Definition component is enabled — reflecting the calculator running on the backend. The user-facing surface stays consistent; the underlying logic switches transparently.

Inline bookmarks

Save Bookmark sits adjacent to Compute. Bookmarks open in the same surface, not in a separate dialog — supporting the configure-once, run-daily workflow analysts had been recreating in Excel.

Configurable field visibility

A per-tab settings panel lets analysts hide controls they don't use — keeping advanced fields available for power users without cluttering the default view for occasional ones.

VaR scenario — adaptive components, Advanced Fields surfaced for power-user controls.
Save Bookmark — inline, with the existing bookmark list visible alongside.

What users said held up after launch

Eight pages collapsed into three. Sales agents stopped scrolling for service categories they never quoted. The recall load that ramping new joiners had been struggling with disappeared with it.

Inline validation replaced trial and error. Errors that the legacy form only surfaced on submit now showed at the field. Quotes that used to bounce back from finance for missing data went through first time more often.

Mobile-responsive replaced desktop-only. Agents quoting from a customer site no longer needed to be back at a desk. The pre-responsive legacy layout had forced field visits to end before quote generation could start.

Familiar from day one. Sales agents recognised the patterns from consumer cloud tools they already used. Internal training time on the new platform dropped against the legacy ramp.

The +23 SUS lift wasn't the interesting number.

The four dimensions that moved most were learnability and consistency — symptoms of the same root cause. Agents had been holding the location of every service category in memory because the form gave them no other way to find it. Three sequenced steps removed that load, and the SUS score moved with it.

Case 07 (continued) · Platform scope

Eight modules, one design system, four user tiers

The platform had to handle three concurrent design problems: real GDPR/cybersecurity workflow, a permission model that fit how 9ine actually delivered the service, and a subscription model that let each school enable only the modules they paid for. The design system carried all three.

Modules I designed

01 Governance

Project Groups, Projects, Risks, Issues, Lessons, Tasks. The dashboard surface shown on the previous slide.

02 Privacy & Data Protection

RoPAs, DPIAs, vendor assessments, retention policies. Pre-configured templates for school-specific data processing.

03 Retention Management

GDPR-compliant retention scheduling and destruction logging. Deep-dived on the next slide.

04 Contact Management

Stakeholder records linking owners, members, and notified users to compliance artefacts across the platform.

05 Incident Management

Cyber and data-breach incident reporting with documented response trails and regulator notification timelines.

06 Project Management

Tasks, checklists, Gantt views. Compliance work treated as project work — same primitives as Governance.

07 System Management

EdTech vendor inventory, application catalogue, contract tracking. The 360-degree EdTech management surface.

08 9ine Academy

In-app training. Compliance-officer onboarding for users learning GDPR work for the first time, surfaced on the Home dashboard.

Four-tier permission architecture

The permission model mirrored how 9ine actually delivered the service: schools subscribed module-by-module; each module was role-gated within the school; and 9ine's own consultants had a top-tier admin role to operate alongside their clients.

Tier 1 End User

Access to specific modules within a single school, scoped to their compliance role (e.g. department lead, data champion).

→
Tier 2 School Admin

Manages users, modules, and compliance state for one school. Subscription controls live here.

→
Tier 3 Group Super-admin

Multi-school role for MATs and federation networks. Dashboards aggregate across schools in the group.

→
Tier 4 9ine Consultant

Top-tier admin assigned to a client. Full access to operate the platform alongside the school during onboarding and ongoing engagement.

The strategic point: the platform wasn't a digital version of consulting — it was the structure that let 9ine grow without proportionally hiring consultants. Each module a school enabled was one more thing the consultant didn't have to do manually in Excel. That's the design problem the permission architecture solved.

Case 07 (continued) · Module deep-dive · Retention Management

Retention Management — daily-use GDPR tooling for Data Protection Officers

Retention is a daily compliance task: every personal-data record in a school has a lawful retention period, after which it must be destroyed and the destruction logged. The legacy approach was Excel files maintained per-school by 9ine's consultants. The platform module let the school's own DPO own the work directly.

01 · Main retention list. Compliance officer's primary surface. Sortable across every dimension that matters — type, owner, risk, review date.
02 · Row actions menu. The table is also a workflow hub. One row spawns notes, attachments, tasks, issues, or risks — the same pattern repeated across every module.
03 · New Record · Details. Real GDPR vocabulary — RoPA/DPIA, Retention period, Method of destruction. Fields the DPO needs, not generic SaaS placeholders.
04 · Attachments · edge cases. Three states on one screen — virus scanning in progress, size-validation error, attached files. Unhappy paths designed in from day one.
Honest scoping · founding-designer reality.

Discovery was lighter than my agency or in-house work. Inputs were DPO interviews, the consultants' existing Excel templates, and direct usage by the founding consultants who became the platform's first internal testers. Designs went through DPO review pre-launch but not the SUS-driven validation cycles I ran on Cases 01-06. The compounding test was deployment — the platform now serves schools globally, and the modules I designed are still the spine of the product (visible on 9ine.com today).
